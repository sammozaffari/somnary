# Somnary Decision Frameworks And Operating System

## The Core Decision Question

Every product, content, AI, growth, design, and monetization decision should answer:

> Does this make Somnary more useful as an accountable evidence layer, or does it make Somnary more like the sleep-marketing internet it exists to correct?

If the decision weakens the evidence layer, do not ship it.

## Editorial Decision Framework

Ship content when all are true:

- the user problem is real and specific;
- the claim can be stated plainly;
- the evidence can be graded using the existing rubric;
- safety boundaries are visible;
- sources are real and checked;
- the page helps a user make a better decision without telling them what to take.

Do not ship when:

- the topic is only interesting but not decision-relevant;
- the claim depends on mechanism-only evidence;
- the page would require personalized medical advice;
- the headline outruns the evidence;
- the monetization incentive would compromise trust.

## Content Prioritization Score

Score each content idea 1-5 on:

- user urgency;
- search/social demand;
- risk-reduction value;
- evidence availability;
- differentiation from generic AI;
- reuse across channels;
- newsletter potential;
- professional backlink potential.

Prioritize ideas scoring high on urgency, risk reduction, and differentiation.

## Article Framework

Every article should include:

1. Bottom line.
2. Who this applies to.
3. What people claim.
4. What human evidence shows.
5. What the evidence does not show.
6. Dose/form/label reality where relevant.
7. Safety boundary.
8. What to ask a clinician or pharmacist.
9. Sources.
10. Review date and correction link.

## Advertisement Framework

An ad is allowed only if:

- the claim is source-backed;
- the landing page contains the substantiation;
- the ad does not imply treatment/cure;
- the ad does not imply personalized advice;
- the CTA matches the user's intent;
- the creative does not use fear without action;
- the disclosure is clear if there is any material connection.

Best CTA verbs:

- Check
- Compare
- Read
- Understand
- Spot

Avoid CTA verbs:

- Cure
- Fix
- Treat
- Optimize your dose
- Build your stack

## Design Decision Framework

A design change should improve one of:

- time-to-understanding;
- decision clarity;
- source trust;
- safety visibility;
- comparison speed;
- accessibility;
- editorial distinctiveness.

Design should avoid:

- wellness clichés;
- supplement-store aesthetics;
- decorative dream imagery that reduces seriousness;
- hidden disclaimers;
- grade colors that imply “safe for everyone”;
- UI patterns that make weak evidence feel strong.

## AI Recommendation Framework

AI recommendations should never be framed as “you should take X.”

Allowed framing:

- “Somnary grades X as B for this use case.”
- “The evidence suggests a modest effect in this population.”
- “This is not well studied for your stated goal.”
- “This safety issue means you should talk to a clinician or pharmacist.”

Forbidden framing:

- “Take X tonight.”
- “Your ideal dose is Y.”
- “This is safe for you.”
- “Combine these three supplements.”
- “You probably have sleep apnea/anxiety/insomnia.”

## Product Decision Framework

Prioritize product features that increase checked decisions:

- remedy checker;
- label checker;
- compare tool;
- safety router;
- clinician handout;
- evidence change log.

Deprioritize features that increase open-ended advice:

- general sleep chatbot;
- community dosing threads;
- supplement stack builder;
- marketplace before QA maturity;
- affiliate “best product” pages.

## Collaboration Decision Framework

Approve collaboration when:

- the collaborator improves evidence, safety, distribution, or professional trust;
- the relationship is disclosed;
- the collaborator cannot influence grades;
- the output is useful without buying anything.

Reject collaboration when:

- a supplement brand funds the review;
- a partner wants grade influence;
- the content becomes a disguised ad;
- the collaborator's audience expects individualized medical advice.

## Consistency Checklist

Before publishing anything, ask:

- Is the user problem explicit?
- Is the bottom line visible early?
- Is the evidence quality clear?
- Is uncertainty named?
- Are safety boundaries visible?
- Are claims cited?
- Could this be mistaken for medical advice?
- Would this still be credible with no affiliate revenue?
- Is this better than a generic AI answer because of Somnary's method?
