# Melatonin candidate product universe (Phase B) — `[HUMAN-GATE]`

**Status:** candidate list for owner review. Selection is human-gated so it can't
be accused of cherry-picking. Researched 2026-07-17. No product here is scored,
ranked, or judged — this is only *which products we research*.

## Inclusion criteria, applied evenly

- **Primary active = melatonin.** Single-ingredient prioritized; a bounded
  minority of combo products (gummy + botanicals/magnesium/B6) included because
  they are a large share of real consumer purchasing — each labeled `combo`.
- **Market.** AU melatonin is prescription/pharmacist-gated (Schedule 4, S3
  pharmacist exception age 55+), so the consumer OTC universe is overwhelmingly
  US. One AU prescription-grade product (Circadin) is included so the AU market
  is represented, not pretended away.
- **Quality spectrum, evenly reached.** Deliberately spans third-party-certified
  (Nature Made USP, Thorne), practitioner channel (Pure Encapsulations, and
  Douglas Labs as a bench option), mass-market leaders (Natrol, Nature's Bounty,
  NOW, OLLY, Vitafusion), pharmacy house brands (CVS, Walgreens), private label
  (Kirkland), and the high-dose gummy segment (Nature's Truth 10 mg) that
  independent testing has historically flagged — included precisely so the set
  is not stacked toward "clean" products.
- **Forms.** Tablet, extended/prolonged-release, capsule, gummy, and liquid all
  represented. Dose range spans physiologic (0.3 mg) to high (10 mg).

## Deliberate exclusions (documented)

- **Kirkland Signature Sleep Aid (doxylamine 25 mg)** — an antihistamine, not
  melatonin; fails the primary-active test. (The Costco *melatonin* house-brand
  SKU is candidate #8 and must be re-confirmed live — that SKU rotates.)
- **Pediatric gummies (OLLY Kids, ZzzQuil Kids 0.5 mg)** — excluded to keep the
  set adult-facing; children's melatonin is a conservative, separately-scoped
  topic (non-negotiable: be conservative on children).
- **Additional ZzzQuil / P&G gummy variants** — excluded as redundant; the
  high-dose and botanical-combo gummy spectrum points are already covered, and
  adding more would over-weight one segment.

## Note for the owner
Circadin (#14) is a registered medicine, not a supplement — its scorecard would
read differently (it has an assessment report, an ARTG entry, an SmPC). Keeping
it is valuable as the regulated counterpoint, but flag if you'd rather scope the
pilot to US OTC supplements only and hold Circadin for a "prescription options"
treatment.
