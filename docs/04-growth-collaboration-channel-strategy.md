# Growth, Collaboration, Community, And Channel Strategy

## Growth Thesis

Somnary should not compete with generic sleep advice. It should compete with misleading certainty.

The repeatable growth hook is:

> We checked the claim.

This works across SEO, YouTube, TikTok, Instagram, X.com, newsletter, and collaborations.

## Newsletter Strategy

Newsletter name options:

- Sleep Claims Watch
- The Somnary Evidence Note
- Before You Take It
- Claim vs Data

Recommended format: weekly, concise, one claim per issue.

Issue template:

1. The claim.
2. The short answer.
3. What the best human evidence says.
4. What the label or market gets wrong.
5. Who should be careful.
6. The Somnary verdict.
7. Send us a claim or label.

Newsletter lead magnets:

- “The 7 sleep supplement label traps”
- “Melatonin dose and timing cheat sheet”
- “Questions to ask a pharmacist before taking sleep supplements”
- “Sleep blend checklist”

## YouTube Channel Angles

Channel positioning: evidence-led consumer protection for sleep remedies.

Primary series:

1. Claim vs Data
   - “Does magnesium actually help sleep?”
   - “Does CBD help insomnia, or is it THC doing the work?”
   - “Does valerian deserve its reputation?”

2. Label Teardowns
   - “This sleep blend hides the dose.”
   - “Why proprietary blends are a red flag.”
   - “What 10mg melatonin gummies get wrong.”

3. Sleep Myth Court
   - opening claim;
   - evidence presented;
   - cross-examination;
   - verdict.

4. Ask A Pharmacist / Ask A Sleep Clinician
   - collaborative interviews with strict non-personalized boundaries.

5. The Strongest Evidence Is Not A Supplement
   - CBT-I explainers, digital CBT-I, sleep restriction caution, when to seek care.

YouTube style:

- skeptical but not smug;
- simple evidence graphics;
- no fearmongering thumbnails;
- direct titles that match search intent.

Recommended title patterns:

- “Melatonin is a clock signal, not a sleeping pill”
- “The sleep supplement label trick nobody reads”
- “CBD for sleep: the evidence is weaker than the reputation”
- “Why CBT-I beats sleep supplements for chronic insomnia”

## Instagram Strategy

Role: saved/shareable education.

Formats:

- carousel: “Claim / Evidence / Verdict”
- carousel: “Before you take X”
- static card: grade and one-sentence translation
- reel: quick label teardown
- story: poll on sleep claim, then evidence answer

Instagram rules:

- make every post savable;
- use citations in caption or final slide;
- avoid miracle/before-after language;
- avoid implying individualized advice;
- pin medical boundary highlights.

## TikTok Strategy

Role: reach and fast myth correction.

Formats:

- 30-45 second “claim check” videos;
- green-screen label breakdowns;
- “things I would check before taking this”;
- “sleep supplement red flags in 20 seconds”;
- “grade reveal” videos.

Tone:

- fast, clear, slightly adversarial against bad claims;
- never mocking the user for trying something;
- punch up at marketing, not down at tired people.

TikTok hooks:

- “This label tells you almost nothing.”
- “If your melatonin gummy says 10mg, here is the problem.”
- “Natural sleep aid does not mean safe sleep aid.”
- “This is why we grade valerian a C.”

## X.com Strategy

Role: credibility, evidence threads, journalists, clinicians, search-adjacent discovery.

Formats:

- source threads;
- “paper changed / grade unchanged” posts;
- screenshot label teardowns;
- correction log updates;
- calls for reader claims.

Thread pattern:

1. Claim in plain English.
2. Why people believe it.
3. Best evidence.
4. Limitation.
5. Safety boundary.
6. Somnary verdict.
7. Link to full page.

X.com should be less lifestyle and more evidence-room. It is a place to earn trust from skeptical professionals and writers.

## Collaboration Strategy

Best collaborators:

- pharmacists;
- sleep clinicians;
- CBT-I therapists;
- pediatricians for child-melatonin boundaries;
- consumer-protection journalists;
- evidence-based medicine creators;
- supplement-testing labs;
- digital CBT-I providers, with careful disclosure.

Collaboration formats:

- co-authored safety guides;
- expert-reviewed pages;
- YouTube interviews;
- newsletter swaps;
- joint “label teardown” webinars;
- printable handouts for clinics/pharmacies;
- public correction challenges.

Do not collaborate with supplement brands while the trust model is still forming. Free samples, paid reviews, affiliate deals, or “sponsored evidence checks” will confuse the site’s core promise.

## Community Building

Community should be built around claim-checking, not treatment advice.

Allowed community loops:

- submit a claim;
- submit a label;
- report an unclear page;
- vote on next remedy to review;
- share experience as anecdote clearly separated from evidence grade.

Avoid:

- “what should I take?” threads;
- dosage personalization;
- sleep disorder diagnosis;
- unmoderated supplement-stacking communities;
- product recommendation leaderboards unless product QA is legally reviewed.

## Advertising Plan

Start with search and paid social around high-intent problems.

Campaign 1: Melatonin

- landing page: Melatonin Decision Hub
- hooks: clock signal, dose, timing, gummy label accuracy, children.
- avoid: “best melatonin supplement” unless product QA exists.

Campaign 2: Sleep Blends

- landing page: Sleep Blend Label Checker
- hooks: proprietary blends, hidden dose, label traps.

Campaign 3: CBD

- landing page: CBD evidence page
- hooks: CBD vs THC confusion, sleep reputation vs trial evidence.

Campaign 4: CBT-I

- landing page: CBT-I hub
- hooks: strongest evidence is not a supplement.

Ad decision rules:

- every claim must be source-backed;
- do not imply treatment, cure, or guaranteed outcome;
- do not use fear without a concrete safety action;
- route ads to exact landing pages, not the homepage;
- measure newsletter signups and return visits, not only clicks.
