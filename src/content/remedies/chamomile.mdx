chamomile is the sleepy-tea cliché that turns out to have more evidence than the cliché suggests:
just not quite the evidence people assume.

the real support is a meta-analysis. pooling six randomized trials, chamomile significantly improved
subjective sleep quality versus control, with a moderate effect size <CiteMarker n={1} />. that
genuinely sets it apart from most bedtime botanicals, which have nothing of the kind, and it's why
this lands at C rather than a D.

but read what that evidence is *about*. the same meta-analysis found only a single trial testing
chamomile in **insomnia**, and that one showed no significant change. the dedicated trial in people
with diagnosed chronic insomnia agrees: 34 adults on a standardized chamomile extract for a month saw
no significant improvement in their sleep diaries versus placebo <CiteMarker n={2} />. so the
positive signal is for general **sleep quality** (often in special groups like postpartum women and
older adults), not for treating insomnia, and none of it is objective, monitored sleep.

that split is the whole grade. a meta-analysis with a real effect argues for B; the fact that the
effect is subjective quality rather than insomnia, in heterogeneous special populations, with no
objective confirmation, holds it at **C**. next to ashwagandha (also a B-tier meta-analysis, but one
that was *strongest* in diagnosed insomnia), chamomile sits a clear notch lower. a good tea with a
modest, real, and narrow benefit.
