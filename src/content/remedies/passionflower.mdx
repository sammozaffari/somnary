for a herb this popular, the human sleep evidence is startlingly thin: it comes down to
**one small trial**.

that trial randomised 41 healthy adults with mild sleep fluctuations to a week of passionflower
tea or a placebo tea. they rated their sleep quality **slightly better** on passionflower than on
placebo (a real, statistically significant difference), but the objective, brain-wave
(polysomnography) measures **did not change**, and these were people with only minor sleep
complaints, not insomnia <CiteMarker n={1} />.

step back to the whole literature and it doesn't get stronger. a 2020 systematic review pulled
together **nine** passionflower trials and found **only that single sleep study**; everything else
was about anxiety, in small groups of 16 to 128 people. the calming mechanism most often cited
(nudging the brain's GABA system) rests on **rat and test-tube work**, not human sleep outcomes
<CiteMarker n={2} />.

and its best-studied use, anxiety, is barely better served: a cochrane review could find only
**two eligible trials, 198 people in total**, and concluded they were "too few to permit any
conclusions" <CiteMarker n={3} />.

none of this makes passionflower dangerous. short-term use looked well tolerated. but "well
tolerated" is not "works." one small subjective trial, a preclinical mechanism, and a marketing
presence in half the "sleep blends" on the shelf is the textbook shape of a **D**: minimal human
evidence, claims running well ahead of the data.
