reishi (Ganoderma lucidum, lingzhi) is sold for sleep and calm, and it has a centuries-long
traditional reputation for both. but a traditional reputation is not a clinical result, and when you
go looking for the trials, the sleep evidence isn't in people.

it's in rodents. reishi extract increased total and non-REM sleep in rats recorded on EEG
<CiteMarker n={1} />, and in rats and mice it shortened the time to fall asleep alongside pentobarbital —
an effect that disappeared when researchers blocked the benzodiazepine site with flumazenil, pointing
to a GABA-linked, sedative-like mechanism <CiteMarker n={2} />. that's a clean, replicated, plausible
mechanism. it is also entirely animal work.

the human trials exist, but they're about **fatigue, not sleep**. a reishi polysaccharide extract
reduced fatigue and clinician-rated severity in 132 people with neurasthenia over eight weeks
<CiteMarker n={3} />, and a small breast-cancer pilot found less cancer-related fatigue on reishi spore
powder over four weeks <CiteMarker n={4} />. neither enrolled people for a sleep complaint, and neither
measured sleep as a designed outcome — in the cancer pilot, sleep appears only as one symptom item
buried inside a quality-of-life questionnaire. no trial has tested reishi, on its own, in people who
can't sleep. that missing trial is exactly why this is a **D**: the marketing says "sleep", the human
evidence says, at most, "fatigue".

the part the "it's just a mushroom" framing gets wrong is safety. reishi carries a folk reputation as
liver-protective, but the documented cases run the other way: a **fatal fulminant hepatitis** in a
patient who switched from boiled lingzhi to lingzhi **powder** <CiteMarker n={5} />, and an acute liver
injury in a man taking reishi powder with heavy alcohol <CiteMarker n={6} />. these are rare, and
sometimes confounded by other drugs or alcohol — a Cochrane review of cancer trials found reishi
generally well tolerated <CiteMarker n={7} /> — but rare and severe is still severe, and the powdered
concentrate is the form implicated. there's also a theoretical **bleeding** angle: reishi inhibits
platelets in the lab, though a controlled trial in healthy volunteers found no impairment of clotting at
1.5 g/day <CiteMarker n={8} />. that's reassuring for a healthy person and not reassuring at all for
someone on a blood thinner or heading into surgery.

so: a replicated animal mechanism, human trials that are about fatigue rather than sleep, no standalone
human sleep trial at all, and a real — if uncommon — liver signal that the gentle-mushroom marketing
hides. that's a **D**, with the safety box worth reading before the efficacy one.
