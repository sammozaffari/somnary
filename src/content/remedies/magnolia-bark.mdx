Magnolia bark is a case where the laboratory story is more impressive than the human one.

Its active constituents, honokiol and magnolol, act on GABA-A receptors at the benzodiazepine
site: the same target as sedative medicines. In mice, honokiol shortened the time to fall into
non-REM sleep and increased how much non-REM sleep the animals got, and that effect disappeared when
researchers blocked the benzodiazepine site <CiteMarker n={2} />. It's a clean, plausible mechanism.

But plausible in mice is not proven in people, and the human evidence barely touches sleep. The
best human trial tested a **combination** product (magnolia bark plus phellodendron, sold as
"Relora") in moderately stressed adults, and it measured **cortisol and mood, not sleep**
<CiteMarker n={1} />. Magnolia bark on its own has never been put through a standalone human sleep
trial; where it appears, it's folded into stress or menopause formulas whose individual ingredients
can't be separated out.

So the honest picture is a strong preclinical mechanism, a combination-only human record that isn't
even about sleep, and no standalone trial at all. That is a **D**: the claims run well ahead of the
human data.
