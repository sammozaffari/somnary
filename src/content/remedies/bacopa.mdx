Bacopa monnieri — brahmi, in ayurveda — is one of the better-studied herbs on the nootropic shelf.
That's exactly why its appearance in "calm" and "sleep" blends is misleading: the research that makes
bacopa look legitimate is not about sleep.

What the good trials actually measured was **cognition**. A meta-analysis of controlled trials found
bacopa modestly sped up attention and reaction time <CiteMarker n={2} />, and a systematic review of
six 12-week trials found improvements in memory recall <CiteMarker n={3} />. These are real, if
modest, effects — on cognitive tests. A separate trial in older adults added a signal for reduced
**anxiety** and low mood <CiteMarker n={4} />. None of these are sleep outcomes, and none were fast:
bacopa's effects build over about 12 weeks, which is the opposite of what people want from a bedtime
aid.

So what happens when you test bacopa for sleep directly? Someone did. A 28-day trial gave 100 adults
with poor sleep a standardized extract or placebo, and its **primary outcome was a validated insomnia
scale**. Bacopa did not beat placebo on that sleep score <CiteMarker n={1} />. It did edge out placebo
on some emotional-wellbeing and general-health measures — consistent with the anxiety/mood story — but
not on sleep itself. That is the whole case in one study: the calming reputation shows up, the sleep
benefit does not.

That's why this is a **D and not an F**. An F would mean no human data, or a debunked, harmful claim.
Bacopa has genuine human trials — they just measure the wrong thing for our purposes, and the one time
sleep was the endpoint, the result was null. The grade reflects a category gap, not an absence of
science. If you see bacopa sold *for sleep*, the honest read is that the claim is borrowed from
cognition and anxiety work, not earned on sleep.

On safety, bacopa is generally well tolerated, but with real caveats. Its most common side effect is
**GI upset** — cramps, nausea, loose stools — a dose-related, cholinergic effect that's worse on an
empty stomach <CiteMarker n={6} />. Because it's cholinergic and inhibits several drug-metabolizing
enzymes, it can interact with cholinergic/anticholinergic drugs and others <CiteMarker n={6} />. Animal
data show it can raise the thyroid hormone T4 by around 40% <CiteMarker n={5} />, and a recent review
flags **thyroid conditions and medication** as a caution and notes that pregnancy and breastfeeding
safety isn't established <CiteMarker n={7} />. And as a botanical, dose honesty matters: the human
trials used specific standardized extracts, while blends usually hide how much bacopa (and how much
bacoside) you're actually getting — and no studied dose was shown to improve sleep in the first place.

If you're considering bacopa for stress or memory, that's a different conversation to have with a
clinician or pharmacist — especially about thyroid and any medicines you take. For **sleep**, the
direct evidence isn't there.
