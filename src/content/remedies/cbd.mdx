CBD is one of the few remedies here where the popular belief and the controlled evidence point in
opposite directions. and the evidence wins.

when researchers pooled the randomized trials, cannabinoids as a class did modestly improve sleep
quality. but split them apart and the effect belonged to the **THC-type** cannabinoids. **CBD on its
own showed no significant effect at all** <CiteMarker n={1} />. a randomized pilot then tested CBD
directly in people with moderate-to-severe insomnia: 150 mg a night for two weeks did **not** beat
placebo on insomnia severity, on how fast they fell asleep, on sleep efficiency, or on time awake in
the night. what did move was their sense of well-being: the authors concluded the effect was
psychological rather than a real sleep benefit <CiteMarker n={2} />. and a review of the wider field
found only a couple of studies that isolate CBD for insomnia at all; most of the encouraging data
comes from CBD-with-THC products <CiteMarker n={3} />.

so where does CBD's sleep reputation come from? from THC-containing cannabinoids that are a different
thing, from relief of anxiety or pain that lets sleep follow, from feeling generally better, and from
placebo, not from CBD acting on sleep. that's a **D**.

two practical cautions on top of the null result. first, you often don't get what the label says:
independent testing found most online CBD products were mislabeled, some with THC in them
<CiteMarker n={4} />. second (and this is a legal fact, separate from the evidence), in **Australia**
CBD is effectively prescription-only. the TGA created a pathway in 2021 for low-dose CBD to be sold
by a pharmacist without a prescription, but no products have been approved that qualify, so in
practice you need a prescription. none of that changes the grade, which is about whether it works;
it's just part of the honest picture.
