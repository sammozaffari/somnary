l-tryptophan is a case where the honest specifics matter more than the headline. yes, there's a
meta-analysis, and yes, it's positive. but read exactly what it says.

pooling four studies, a systematic review and meta-analysis found that 1 gram or more of tryptophan
before bed significantly **shortened the time people spent awake after falling asleep**, and the
authors judged it could improve sleep quality. but the same analysis found it did **not** help people
fall asleep faster, and did not change total sleep time <CiteMarker n={1} />. so the real, measured
benefit is about staying asleep, at a meaningful dose: not the drift-off aid it's often sold as. and
none of the pooled studies were in people with diagnosed insomnia.

older sleep-lab work, from the 1980s, did report that tryptophan cut sleep-onset time in some younger
"situational" insomniacs <CiteMarker n={2} />. but that's dated, and it points at the one outcome the
modern meta-analysis didn't confirm. a real effect, narrowly defined, on a thin evidence base: that's
a C.

the safety section is where tryptophan earns careful reading despite its "it's in turkey" image. as a
serotonin precursor, it can cause **serotonin syndrome** if combined with SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, or
migraine triptans (a lower potency than concentrated 5-HTP because of an extra conversion step, but
the same real risk). and there's history: in 1989 a **contaminated** batch of tryptophan caused
eosinophilia-myalgia syndrome, a serious and sometimes fatal illness, which is why it was pulled from
the US market for years <CiteMarker n={3} />. the molecule itself wasn't the culprit. the
contaminant was. but that's exactly why you want a third-party-tested, pharmaceutical-grade product.
