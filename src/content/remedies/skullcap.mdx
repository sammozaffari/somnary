skullcap is the closest call in this catalog. it lands at D for a reason worth spelling out,
because the reason is exactly what this site is for.

there is a sleep trial. in 2025, 66 people with poor sleep took an American skullcap extract for eight
weeks and reported better sleep-quality scores than on placebo <CiteMarker n={1} />. on its own, that
would lift skullcap out of the bottom tier. but look at the trial. it was single-center. the product
was supplied by its manufacturer, and one of the authors works in the industry. no independent group
has repeated it. and here's what decides it. the authors reported a **carryover effect** and
noted the washout period between treatments was too short. in a crossover trial, that's not a footnote:
it means the second phase was contaminated by the first, which undermines the comparison the whole
result depends on. a single, manufacturer-supplied, unreplicated trial with a compromised design
doesn't demonstrate a benefit, no matter that the number reached significance. the other human trial
isn't even about sleep: it improved mood in healthy volunteers but did nothing for anxiety
<CiteMarker n={2} />.

then there's what "skullcap" even means, which is really two things. **American skullcap** (*Scutellaria
lateriflora*) is the one with the weak sleep trial. and it has a documented habit of being adulterated
with **germander**, an unrelated plant that contains hepatotoxic compounds; that adulteration, not the
herb itself, is the main reason skullcap has been tied to liver injury <CiteMarker n={3} />. **Chinese
skullcap** (*Scutellaria baicalensis*) is a different species used differently, and it has its own
liver-injury case reports, including one that came back when the person resumed taking it
<CiteMarker n={4} />. so the safety message isn't "this herb is toxic". it's "know which species
you're taking and buy it verified and unadulterated."

a compromised single trial, no replication, and a sourcing problem: that's a **D**.
