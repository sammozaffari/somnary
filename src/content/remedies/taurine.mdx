Taurine is the rare supplement sold to do two opposite things: to calm you down for sleep, and — as
the headline ingredient in energy drinks — to wake you up. That contradiction is the fastest way to
see what's actually going on.

Start with the calming claim, because it isn't baseless. Taurine is one of the most abundant free
amino acids in the brain, it's structurally close to the inhibitory neurotransmitters GABA and
glycine, and it acts as an agonist at GABA-A receptors — the same calming system sedative drugs work
on <CiteMarker n={1} />. Crucially, and unlike GABA taken by mouth, taurine is naturally present in the brain. So a
relaxing, sleep-adjacent effect is genuinely plausible. And in the lab it shows up: in fruit flies,
taurine raised total sleep by about half, while caffeine cut it — the two ingredients of an energy
drink pulling in opposite directions <CiteMarker n={2} />.

Here's the wall. That's an insect. When you look for the human sleep trial — the standalone,
placebo-controlled study of taurine for falling or staying asleep — it isn't there. What taurine
*does* have is a serious human evidence base pointed somewhere else entirely: a meta-analysis of 25
randomized trials found it modestly lowers blood pressure and blood glucose <CiteMarker n={4} />. That
is real human data, and it is not about sleep. Reading a blood-pressure result as a sleep result is
exactly the move this site exists to stop.

And the energy-drink paradox resolves cleanly once you separate the molecules. In a standard energy
drink, taurine (around 4000 mg/L) rides along with a substantial dose of caffeine <CiteMarker n={5} />.
The "energy" is the caffeine. Taurine is not the stimulant — but it also isn't a demonstrated
sedative. It's a well-tolerated amino acid <CiteMarker n={3} /> that happens to travel in stimulant
company, which is precisely why people credit it with effects that belong to the caffeine beside it.

So the grade. Taurine is graded **F** — not because it has been disproven, and not for a safety
problem, but because the grade tracks published *human sleep* evidence and taurine has none: there
is no standalone human sleep trial at all. The mechanism is real and taurine is present in the brain,
but a mechanism and an animal signal are not a human result. That puts it below even the D-tier
remedies like glycine and gaba, which at least have *some* weak human sleep trial to point to. We
don't let a plausible mechanism or non-sleep human trials read as human sleep efficacy — so "taurine
helps you sleep" is not something the human evidence currently supports.
