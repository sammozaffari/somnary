Ashwagandha is one of the few botanicals on this site with genuine meta-analytic backing, which is
what earns it a B. But the "gentle adaptogen" framing skips some real cautions.

The benefit first. A meta-analysis of five randomized trials found a small-to-moderate improvement in
sleep, with a standardized mean difference of −0.59, and it was clearest in people actually diagnosed
with insomnia and at doses of 600 mg or more taken for at least eight weeks <CiteMarker n={1} />. A
ten-week trial backs that up, with a gradual improvement in how quickly people fell asleep and how
efficiently they slept <CiteMarker n={2} />. So this is a real, repeatable effect — but a modest one
that **builds over weeks**, not a first-night sedative.

Now the parts the marketing tends to omit. There are documented cases of ashwagandha-associated
**liver injury**: five patients with cholestatic injury and jaundice in one case series, rare but
real <CiteMarker n={3} />. It can also **raise thyroid hormone levels**: a randomized trial in people
with subclinical hypothyroidism found it significantly increased T3 and T4 and lowered TSH
<CiteMarker n={4} />, which is a real consideration if you have thyroid disease or take thyroid
medication. And it should be **avoided in pregnancy**. None of this makes ashwagandha dangerous for
most healthy adults. But "natural" is not the same as "no cautions," and a B grade is honest about
both halves.
