Valerian is the test of whether this site means what it says. It is the most famous natural sleep
aid, it's graded up almost everywhere, and the honest reading of the evidence is a careful **C**.

The case *for* it is real but soft. One meta-analysis found people were about 1.8 times as likely to
report improved sleep on valerian than on placebo, except the same authors flagged that most of the
studies had significant methodological problems and that there was evidence of **publication bias**,
the pattern where small negative trials quietly go missing <CiteMarker n={1} />. A second
meta-analysis of eighteen trials found a subjective improvement too, but noted the **objective**
measures (how long people actually took to fall asleep) barely moved <CiteMarker n={2} />.

Why so inconsistent? Largely because "valerian" isn't one thing. A 2020 review tied the conflicting
results to the wildly variable quality of the extracts, and suggested the whole root may be more
reliable than heavily processed products <CiteMarker n={3} />. And the tie-breaker: the single most
rigorous synthesis, pooling fourteen trials, found that **no** herbal medicine it examined (valerian
included) beat placebo on any of thirteen efficacy measures <CiteMarker n={4} />.

So the signal shrinks as the studies get better. That's not an F. Valerian has a genuine human trial
base and a clean short-term safety record, which is more than most botanicals here can claim. But a
subjective effect, undercut by publication bias and absent from the objective data, in a remedy far
more popular than its evidence, is the textbook definition of **C**. We won't grade it up for being
famous.
