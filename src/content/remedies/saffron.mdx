saffron earns its B honestly: it has more than one meta-analysis behind it, an objective biological
signal, and a plausible mechanism — sitting alongside the caveats of modest effect sizes,
questionnaire-heavy outcomes, and industry funding.

start with the pooled evidence. a meta-analysis of randomized trials found saffron improved the
Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index by about **2.1 points** and the Insomnia Severity Index by about
**2.6 points** versus placebo, both statistically significant <CiteMarker n={4} />. a separate
systematic review of five randomized trials across three countries reached the same direction —
better sleep duration and quality — while framing the evidence as "initial support" rather than
settled <CiteMarker n={2} />. and a dedicated insomnia meta-analysis of eight trials found reduced
insomnia severity and better sleep quality, but was explicit that this rests on **very-low to
moderate quality** evidence and that several trials were **industry-funded** <CiteMarker n={3} />.

the part that lifts saffron above questionnaire-only remedies is a biomarker. in a 120-person
randomized trial, an evening dose of standardized saffron **raised evening melatonin** versus placebo
without changing cortisol, with 14 mg and 28 mg working about equally well <CiteMarker n={5} />. that
is a measured biological change, not just how people said they slept. saffron's mood effect is
independently large versus placebo in meta-analysis <CiteMarker n={6} />, which is why part of the
sleep benefit may run through mood — and why it warrants care alongside antidepressants.

now the honest limits. the same insomnia meta-analysis found **no significant change in objective
sleep latency or efficiency**; the strongest, most consistent effects are on subjective quality
<CiteMarker n={3} />. much of the sleep-specific trial work traces to one research group using a
manufacturer's extract, the effects are modest, and the trials ran weeks, not months. saffron is
also **not consequence-free**: therapeutic doses (~30 mg) look safe, but high doses are toxic, and a
toxicology review documents abortifacient and teratogenic effects at high doses plus a
miscarriage signal in saffron-field workers — so it should be **avoided in pregnancy**
<CiteMarker n={7} />. taken together, that is a real, repeated, biomarker-backed but modest effect on
a partly industry-funded base: a defensible **B**, not an A.
