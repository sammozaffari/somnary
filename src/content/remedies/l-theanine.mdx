L-theanine is unusual among calming supplements: it has both a subjective *and* an objective sleep
signal. Just not yet in the people most likely to search for it.

The subjective side comes from a four-week randomized crossover trial in stressed adults taking
200 mg a day. Their overall sleep-quality scores improved significantly versus placebo, including
how quickly they fell asleep and how disturbed their sleep was, although sleep was a **secondary**
outcome in what was really a stress study <CiteMarker n={1} />.

The more interesting evidence is objective. In a randomized trial of boys with ADHD, 400 mg a day
produced significantly better sleep on a wrist activity monitor (higher sleep efficiency and a
higher proportion of time actually asleep), even though the time to fall asleep didn't change
<CiteMarker n={2} />. An actigraphy signal is harder to fake than a questionnaire, and it's what
lifts L-theanine above the remedies whose only evidence is how people *say* they slept.

The honest limits: those two populations (stressed adults and ADHD children) aren't the general
insomniac, sleep was often a secondary measure, and there's no large trial in diagnosed insomnia.
So this is a **B**: a real, repeatable, well-tolerated effect on a thin, specific evidence base.
Not the knock-out the label implies, but not nothing either.
