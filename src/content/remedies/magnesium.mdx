The human evidence for magnesium and sleep is best described as **promising but thin**.

The anchor is a 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis of oral magnesium for insomnia in older
adults. It found only **three randomised trials, 151 people in total**, across three countries.
Pooled, sleep-onset latency was **17.36 minutes shorter** than placebo (95% CI −27.27 to −7.44), and
that gap was statistically significant. Total sleep time improved by about sixteen minutes but was **not**
significant. Crucially, the authors judged **all three trials at moderate-to-high risk of bias** and
rated the certainty of evidence **low to very low**; their own words are that the literature is
"substandard for physicians to make well-informed recommendations" <CiteMarker n={1} />.

Since then the picture has been strengthened, modestly, by a **2025 randomised, double-blind,
placebo-controlled trial** of magnesium bisglycinate (250 mg elemental magnesium daily) in 155
adults reporting poor sleep, the **largest placebo-controlled magnesium-and-sleep trial to date**.
It found a significantly greater drop in the insomnia severity index than placebo (−3.9 vs −2.3
points at four weeks, p=0.049), with a **small effect size** (Cohen's d ≈ 0.2), and a hint that
people with **low baseline dietary magnesium** benefited most <CiteMarker n={2} />.

Put together: a real, repeatable, but **small** effect (strongest in older adults and the
low-magnesium), resting on a small and largely low-quality evidence base. That is exactly what a
B looks like: real human signal, thin foundation.
