For chronic insomnia, CBT-I isn't one option among many. It's the one every major guideline puts
first. The American College of Physicians recommends that **all** adults receive CBT-I as the
initial treatment, before any sleeping pill <CiteMarker n={1} />, and the American Academy of Sleep
Medicine gives multicomponent CBT-I its single **strongest** recommendation <CiteMarker n={2} />.
Two independent bodies, the same conclusion.

The numbers behind that are unusually consistent. A meta-analysis of 20 randomized trials found
CBT-I shortened the time to fall asleep by about 19 minutes, cut time awake during the night by
about 26 minutes, and raised sleep efficiency by nearly 10 percent, with what the authors called
clinically meaningful effect sizes <CiteMarker n={3} />. A much larger synthesis of 87 trials put
the effect on insomnia severity itself in the **large** range, and it held up across different
populations and in people with other health conditions, not just isolated insomnia
<CiteMarker n={4} />.

The honest catch is what CBT-I does *not* do: it consolidates sleep rather than lengthening it. The
strong gains are in falling asleep faster, staying asleep, and sleep efficiency; **total sleep time
barely moves**, and that pooled estimate wasn't even statistically significant <CiteMarker n={3} />.
That's by design. The sleep-restriction step deliberately trims time in bed at first, so early on
you may sleep a little less and feel sleepier by day before things consolidate. Anyone expecting a
couple of extra hours is measuring the wrong thing.

So if it works this well, why doesn't everyone do it? The barrier isn't the evidence, it's access
and effort. CBT-I takes a trained therapist and a few weeks of consistent practice, and trained
providers are scarce. That's where digital CBT-I comes in: a randomized trial of an online
programme in more than 1,700 people improved sleep-related quality of life, functional health, and
well-being against basic sleep-hygiene advice, with the gains still there at the six-month
follow-up <CiteMarker n={5} />. It doesn't replace a therapist for complex cases, but it puts the
best-evidenced treatment within reach at a scale clinics alone never could.
