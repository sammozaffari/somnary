  With iron the trap is elemental content and documented need, not a botanical extract. "325 mg

mechanism: >-
  Iron is a cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme in dopamine synthesis, and
  brain-iron deficiency is thought to be central to RLS pathophysiology — with reduced iron in the
  substantia nigra sometimes present even when blood iron looks normal, and downstream changes in
  dopaminergic and adenosine signaling. Correcting low iron is therefore thought to address a root
  cause of RLS rather than to sedate. This is why the benefit is tied to deficiency: there is no
  proposed mechanism by which extra iron would improve sleep in someone whose iron stores are already
  adequate, and mechanism is not the same as proof.

Read the scope before anything else: **iron is not a sleep aid.** It earns a B here for one narrow,
well-defined job — quieting **restless legs syndrome (RLS) that is being driven by low iron.** If your
sleep is broken by an urge to move your legs at night and your **ferritin is low**, correcting that
deficiency is a legitimate, guideline-backed treatment. If your iron is already normal, iron does
nothing for your sleep, and taking it anyway is not benign.

The evidence in scope is genuinely solid. A 2019 **Cochrane review** concluded that iron therapy
**probably improves RLS severity** versus placebo, at moderate certainty <CiteMarker n={3} />. Two
clinical-practice guidelines — the **IRLSSG** task-force report <CiteMarker n={1} /> and the **AASM**
guideline <CiteMarker n={2} /> — both recommend iron, but only against measured iron status: oral iron
when ferritin is roughly **≤75 µg/L**, IV iron when it's **≤100 µg/L**. On the trial side, a
placebo-controlled RCT of **oral ferrous sulfate** in people with low-normal ferritin cut RLS scores
sharply over 12 weeks <CiteMarker n={6} />, and the strongest signal is for **IV ferric
carboxymaltose**: a 2024 multicenter RCT (n=209) beat placebo on RLS severity <CiteMarker n={4} />,
which is why the AASM gives it a *strong* recommendation. Note the timing, though — a 2017 IV trial
only reached significance at **12 weeks**, not 4 <CiteMarker n={5} />. This is slow correction of a
deficiency, not an overnight fix.

## What the evidence does not show

It does **not** show that iron is a sedative, and it does **not** show any sleep benefit in people
whose iron is already normal. Every trial that worked pre-selected people with **low or low-normal
ferritin**; the mechanism (iron as a cofactor for dopamine synthesis, and brain-iron deficiency in
RLS) explains why there's simply no reason to expect benefit once stores are adequate. So "take iron
to sleep better" is not a claim the evidence supports — the honest claim is "correct a documented iron
deficiency that is driving RLS."

## The safety line is real

Iron has a serious toxicity profile at both ends. **Acute overdose is a leading cause of fatal
poisoning in young children** — adult and prenatal tablets can reach a toxic dose fast — so iron must
be **locked away from children** <CiteMarker n={7} />. At the other end, taking iron *without* a
documented deficiency risks **iron overload**, which is especially dangerous in hereditary
hemochromatosis and damages the liver, heart, and pancreas <CiteMarker n={7} />. This is why the one
non-negotiable step is a **clinician-checked ferritin/iron panel before you start** — this page does
not tell you a dose. Bring the RLS and the ferritin question to a clinician or pharmacist, and let the
test, not a supplement label, decide.
