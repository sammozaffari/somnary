The single most useful thing to know about lavender and sleep is that "lavender" means two very
different products with very different evidence, and the popular one is the weaker one.

Start with the strong half. Oral lavender oil, sold as the standardized preparation Silexan, is a
genuinely well-evidenced anti-anxiety treatment. A meta-analysis of five double-blind, randomized,
placebo-controlled trials (over twelve hundred people) found it significantly better than placebo
for anxiety, and in one trial it held its own against the antidepressant paroxetine <CiteMarker n={1} />.
That's a real result. And when researchers gave oral Silexan to anxious people who were also sleeping
badly, their sleep-quality scores improved too <CiteMarker n={2} />. But note *why*: disturbed sleep
was studied alongside anxiety, and the sleep got better as the anxiety got better. It's a knock-on
effect, not a direct sleep drug, and it's never been tested in diagnosed insomnia.

Now the weak half: the one most people mean by "lavender for sleep." Pillow sprays, diffusers, and
essential oils have far thinner evidence, and they carry a problem no study can fully solve: you can
*smell* lavender, so participants know whether they got the real thing. That makes a proper
placebo-controlled trial nearly impossible, and it means a good chunk of any reported benefit could
be expectation rather than the oil.

So lavender lands at **C**: a real, meta-analysis-backed anxiolytic whose sleep benefit is secondary,
attached to a popular aromatherapy form whose evidence is weak and hard to trust. If anxiety is what
keeps you up, the oral standardized oil has a reasonable case. The diffuser is pleasant. Just don't
mistake pleasant for proven.
