Apigenin has a marketing problem disguised as a selling point. It's pitched as "the active compound in
chamomile" and as a lab-verified GABA-A receptor binder, and both of those things are true. What
neither of them is: evidence that swallowing isolated apigenin improves human sleep.

Start with the emptiness, because it's the whole story. We found no adequate standalone human sleep
trial of apigenin by itself. A 2024 review of apigenin's sleep and aging biology walks through the
animal work and the human chamomile studies, then says the quiet part directly — clinical studies are
still needed to test apigenin "on its own, as opposed to apigenin in chamomile extract"
<CiteMarker n={1} />. That's the state of the field: a popular supplement with essentially no
dedicated human sleep data.

So what are people actually pointing to when they say apigenin works? Chamomile. And here the category
error matters. Chamomile is a whole-plant extract with many constituents, and apigenin is only about
1% of it — so a chamomile trial can't tell you what apigenin contributes on its own. Worse for the
claim: the best-quality chamomile trial in diagnosed insomnia found nothing versus placebo
<CiteMarker n={5} />. Chamomile has its own (modest, subjective, C-grade) evidence, covered on its own
page; borrowing that record for a concentrated apigenin capsule is not a small stretch, it's a
different product.

The mechanism is real but it's doing unearned work. Apigenin genuinely binds the benzodiazepine site
of GABA-A receptors — a Ki around 4 uM — and produced anxiolytic and slight sedative effects in early
rodent studies <CiteMarker n={2} />. That's a plausible calming story. But a review of flavonoids at
GABA-A receptors flags that apigenin's in vivo effects conflict across species — anxiolytic without
sedation in mice, yet sedative and mildly proconvulsant in rats — and involve mechanisms beyond a
simple benzodiazepine action <CiteMarker n={3} />. Biological plausibility is not a human sleep
outcome.

On safety: apigenin is a common food flavonoid and looks low-toxicity in the amounts you'd eat. The
unknown is the concentrated capsule. Supplement-dose safety is essentially unstudied, additive
drowsiness with other sedatives or alcohol is plausible given the GABA-A activity, and in the lab
apigenin inhibits several drug-metabolizing CYP enzymes (for example CYP2C9, Ki ~2 uM)
<CiteMarker n={4} />. That last point is a theoretical interaction flag, not a proven one — but if you
take medicines metabolized by those enzymes, it's worth raising with a pharmacist. Pregnancy,
breastfeeding, and children: unstudied, so avoid and route to a clinician.

That's why this is a **D**, not a C. A C would need genuine, if mixed, human evidence for the thing on
the label. Here the standalone human evidence is absent and the plausibility is borrowed. It's also
why it isn't an F: apigenin isn't disproven or fabricated — the receptor binding is real and the
animal signal exists — it's simply untested in people as an isolated sleep aid. The honest label is
"under-studied," and the claims run far ahead of it.
