CBN has a nickname it hasn't earned. It's sold as "the sleep cannabinoid," the calm, non-intoxicating
part of cannabis that supposedly makes you drowsy. The nickname is older than the evidence, and for a
long time there was essentially no evidence at all.

When a researcher went looking in 2021 for the studies behind the claim, he found none: no clinical
trial had tested CBN against a validated sleep questionnaire or an overnight sleep monitor. The
"sedating" reputation, he concluded, came from **cannabis lore** — the old observation that stale,
aged cannabis makes people sleepy — rather than from CBN itself, and there was "insufficient published
evidence to support sleep-related claims" <CiteMarker n={1} />.

The one study people reach for to prove CBN is sedating actually shows the opposite. In a 1975
experiment, five volunteers were given CBN, THC, and combinations of the two. They felt "drugged,
drunk, dizzy and drowsy" — but **only when CBN was combined with THC**. CBN on its own was no
different from placebo. The drowsiness belonged to the THC <CiteMarker n={2} />. It's a tiny, decades-old
experiment that was never about sleep, and it's been miscited for years as CBN's smoking gun.

## What the newer human trials do — and don't — show

Two randomized human sleep trials have finally appeared, and this is where honesty matters. The
cleaner one tested **20 mg of CBN**, alone and with CBD, in people with disturbed sleep. On its
**primary** outcome — sleep quality — CBN did **not** significantly beat placebo. It did reduce
night-time awakenings and overall sleep disturbance as *secondary* findings, but it didn't touch how
fast people fell asleep or their daytime fatigue, and adding CBD did nothing. The trial was funded by
a cannabinoid manufacturer <CiteMarker n={3} />. A null primary outcome with a couple of positive
secondaries, from a single industry-funded study, is a weak signal — not proof.

The second trial looks more impressive and is weaker than it looks. It was large (about a thousand
people) and found that a purified proprietary CBN improved sleep at every dose, about as well as
melatonin. But it was funded by the product's maker and run by a commercial research contractor, it
relied entirely on a **self-report** questionnaire with no objective sleep measurement, and it tested
a purified isolate that isn't what a generic CBN gummy necessarily contains <CiteMarker n={4} />.

So is there any *objective* evidence CBN increases sleep? Yes — **in rats**. With real sleep
recording, CBN raised total, NREM and REM sleep, with an effect on deep sleep the authors compared to
zolpidem <CiteMarker n={5} />. That's a genuine mechanism lead. But "sedates rats" is exactly where
the CBN story has always been strong and where it has always stalled; animal sedation has a long
history of not translating into human sleep benefit.

## Why this is a D (and whether it's an F)

Put it together: a reputation built on folklore, a famous "proof" that shows the opposite, two
preliminary industry-funded human trials — the stronger of which missed its primary outcome — and one
solid objective result that's in rodents. The marketing is far out ahead of the data. That's a
provisional **D**: the claims massively outrun the evidence.

It's close to an F. The reason it isn't, in this draft's judgment, is that two randomized human trials
now exist and do carry *some* signal, however weak and conflicted — an F should be reserved for a
claim with no credible human evidence at all, or one the controlled evidence actively contradicts.
CBN isn't quite there. But the case is genuinely borderline, and under the house rule — grade down
when the evidence is manufacturer-only and unreplicated — a reviewer who wanted to call it F would be
defensible. **The final grade is a `[HUMAN-GATE]` and is pending owner sign-off.**

Two practical notes, both separate from the grade. The cannabinoid market is poorly regulated and
frequently mislabeled — independent testing of cannabinoid products found most were off their stated
potency, so the CBN dose you're getting may not match the label, and some products carry THC
<CiteMarker n={6} />. And CBN's legal status varies by jurisdiction and can change. Neither changes
whether it works; both are part of the honest picture.
