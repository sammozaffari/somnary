zinc is an unusual entry here: it's not a niche botanical, it's an essential mineral you already need,
and there's a real biological thread tying it to sleep. that thread is why it keeps showing up in
"sleep support" formulas — and why the honest grade is still a D.

start with what's genuinely there. zinc sits in the brain's inhibitory signaling, tuning the GABA,
glycine, and NMDA-receptor systems that gate sleep and wake, and blood zinc levels rise and fall with
sleep <CiteMarker n={5} />. population data backs the association up: in a large cross-sectional
NHANES analysis, serum zinc tracked with sleep duration, with the healthiest 7–8-hour sleepers
carrying the highest levels <CiteMarker n={4} />. and unlike some supplements, zinc has actually been
put through randomized trials — a 2024 systematic review found eight of them <CiteMarker n={1} />.

so why not a higher grade? because "eight trials" hides how thin and scattered the evidence is. the
review's own verdict was "limited but promising": several trials using 30 mg/day or more reported
better sleep, but others — in premenstrual syndrome, in depression — found nothing, and the studies
spanned wildly different groups and doses <CiteMarker n={1} />. that's inconsistency, not a signal.

**what the evidence does not show** is the crux. the single most-cited insomnia trial didn't test
zinc — it tested zinc mixed with melatonin and magnesium in one nightly supplement, and while that
mixture improved sleep scores in older adults, there's no way to know whether the zinc did anything
<CiteMarker n={2} />. melatonin alone would plausibly explain the whole result. standalone zinc trials
do exist — one in 54 ICU nurses found zinc by itself improved sleep scores <CiteMarker n={3} /> — but
that's a small study in one specific occupational group, not evidence that zinc treats insomnia
generally. no trial has established zinc, on its own, as a sleep aid for the average person.

and zinc carries a caveat almost nothing else on this site does: **a real ceiling.** it's an essential
mineral, but chronic high doses (above the adult upper limit of about 40 mg/day of elemental zinc)
crowd out copper and can cause copper deficiency — anemia, low white-cell counts, and nerve damage
that may not fully reverse <CiteMarker n={6} />. several of the sleep trials used doses at or above
that limit. so "more zinc for better sleep" is exactly the wrong instinct: the benefit is unproven and
the overdose risk is real and specific. if you're wondering whether your zinc status is low, that's a
question for a clinician or pharmacist who can check it and weigh it against copper — not a reason to
start piling on a mineral for sleep.
