Tart cherry is the sleep remedy with the most concrete mechanism on this whole site. And it still
lands at C, which tells you something about the gap between mechanism and outcome.

Start with what's genuinely true. Tart cherries contain melatonin, the actual sleep hormone, and this
isn't hand-waving: in a controlled crossover trial, a week of tart-cherry concentrate significantly
raised participants' urinary melatonin and nudged their sleep time and efficiency up on a wrist
monitor <CiteMarker n={2} />. A cherry compound may also spare tryptophan, serotonin's precursor. So
the "it's basically natural melatonin" claim is, unusually, real.

And a couple of small insomnia pilots looked promising: one in just eight older adults reported
about 84 extra minutes of sleep on a sleep study <CiteMarker n={3} />; another reduced time spent
awake in the night <CiteMarker n={4} />. Taken alone, encouraging.

Then you pool it. The one systematic review and meta-analysis of the field combined the studies (of
low-to-moderate quality) and found **no statistically significant difference** between tart cherry
and placebo on sleep efficiency or total sleep time <CiteMarker n={1} />. The individual positives
didn't survive being added together. That's the signature of a **C**: a plausible, even measurable
mechanism, a few small conflicting trials, and a null result when you look at all of them at once. A
pleasant, harmless food with a real melatonin content and, so far, an unproven effect on sleep.
