hops is the classic case of a herb that's famous for sleep but almost never tested on its own.

the standalone human evidence comes down to a single small study: seventeen night-shift nurses
drank a non-alcoholic, hopped beer with dinner for two weeks, and a wrist activity monitor showed
they fell asleep faster than in the control period — about **12 minutes versus 20**
<CiteMarker n={2} />. but that's beer, not a measured dose of hops, in seventeen people: a hint,
not proof.

almost everywhere else, hops rides along with valerian. a review of valerian and hops for primary
insomnia found that while some sleep measures improved, the studies differed so much in design that
the evidence had to be read cautiously, and **proper randomised trials were still needed** before
either could be recommended <CiteMarker n={1} />. the best combination trial — 184 adults on a
standardised valerian-hops extract — found only modest subjective improvement, few results beat
placebo, and **nothing changed on objective sleep** <CiteMarker n={3} />. and by design, a combo
trial can't tell you what hops alone does.

the proposed mechanism — an α-acid breakdown product, 2-methyl-3-buten-2-ol, nudging the GABA
system — is mostly laboratory work <CiteMarker n={2} />. put together: no standalone trial, one
confounded beer study, and a modest combination result. that's a **D** — a traditional sleep herb
whose solo evidence barely exists.
