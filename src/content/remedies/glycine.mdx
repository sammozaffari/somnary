glycine is the best argument on this site for why *who ran the study* belongs in an evidence grade.

the science is genuinely appealing. in rats, glycine eases the onset of sleep by lowering core body
temperature (it widens blood vessels in the skin so the body sheds heat), and it does this by acting
on the brain's master circadian clock; destroy that clock and the effect vanishes <CiteMarker n={2} />.
that's a real, specific mechanism, and it happens to be the same core-temperature drop that a warm
bath before bed uses. the human trials, taken at face value, look encouraging too: better sleep
quality, more daytime alertness.

here's the catch. every one of those human trials comes from Ajinomoto (the company that developed
and sells glycine as a sleep supplement), and the mechanism work is from its research group too. an
**independent** systematic review looked at the human studies and was blunt: they had small samples
and a high risk of bias, they were too heterogeneous to even combine into a meta-analysis, and the
overall evidence is limited <CiteMarker n={1} />. in roughly two decades, no independent group has
reproduced the sleep finding <CiteMarker n={3} />.

on a site whose entire premise is independence and zero brand money, that is the most compromised
kind of evidence there is. an elegant mechanism can't substitute for a result someone other than the
seller has confirmed. so glycine is a **D**: not because it's implausible or unsafe, but because
"it works" currently rests entirely on the word of the company selling it. we grade demonstrated,
independent human benefit, and that's exactly what's missing.
