Melatonin is the most-studied natural sleep aid, and the trials tell a consistent story: a
**real but small** effect. One meta-analysis pooling randomized trials found it shortened the
time to fall asleep by about seven minutes and added roughly eight minutes of total sleep
versus placebo <CiteMarker n={1} />; another, drawing on seventeen trials, put the latency
reduction closer to four minutes with about a two percent gain in sleep efficiency
<CiteMarker n={2} />. Genuinely useful, but nowhere near a prescription sleeping pill.

Where melatonin actually shines is **timing**, not sedation. A systematic review of adult
sleep disorders concluded its most convincing evidence was for reducing sleep-onset latency in
primary insomnia and for circadian problems <CiteMarker n={3} />. In delayed sleep phase
disorder a meta-analysis showed it advanced the body clock by more than an hour
<CiteMarker n={4} />, and a Cochrane review found it "remarkably effective" for jet lag when
crossing five or more time zones, particularly flying east <CiteMarker n={5} />.

The honest counterweight is chronic insomnia. The American Academy of Sleep Medicine reviewed
the same literature and *suggests clinicians not use melatonin* for it: a negative
recommendation, graded weak <CiteMarker n={6} />. So the common marketing ("knocks you out",
"fixes insomnia") runs well ahead of what the evidence supports.

Two practical problems compound this. First, **the bottle may not contain what the label says**:
independent testing of thirty products found melatonin content from 83% below to nearly five
times above the stated dose, and serotonin (a tightly controlled compound) in about a quarter
of them <CiteMarker n={7} />. Second, **more is not better**. Most shelves start at 3–10 mg
when the studied benefit shows up at far lower doses, and the extra mostly buys next-day
grogginess. Melatonin is also not automatically kid-safe: pediatric ingestions climbed 530% in a
decade <CiteMarker n={8} />.
