lemon balm is a real remedy pointed at the wrong target — or, more precisely, sold for sleep when its
evidence is for calm.

what it genuinely does is reduce anxiety. a systematic review and meta-analysis found a
moderate-to-large anti-anxiety effect versus placebo, along with a smaller effect on low mood, and no
serious side effects <CiteMarker n={1} />. that's a legitimate result, and it's the key to reading
everything else about lemon balm and sleep.

the sleep evidence, taken on its own, looks encouraging: a standalone trial in 100 healthy adults who
were stressed and sleeping poorly found that three weeks of a standardised lemon balm extract
significantly improved their sleep-quality scores <CiteMarker n={2} />. but look at what else improved
in the same trial — anxiety, depression, and stress, all at once. when a calming herb improves your
sleep and your anxiety together, in an anxious population, the most likely story is that the better
sleep followed the calm, not that lemon balm is a direct sleep aid. and no trial has tested it in
people with diagnosed insomnia.

that's why it lands at **C** rather than B: the effect is real but secondary, in the wrong population
to call it an insomnia treatment — the same pattern as oral lavender, another anxiolytic whose sleep
benefit rides on its calming effect. if you can't sleep because your mind is racing, lemon balm has a
reasonable case. if your problem is insomnia itself, the evidence isn't there yet.
