GABA is the most intuitive-sounding sleep supplement on the shelf and one of the least proven. and
the two facts are connected.

the intuition: GABA is your brain's main *inhibitory*, calming neurotransmitter, the very system that
sedative drugs act on. so swallowing GABA to calm down and sleep feels like it must work. the
problem is that a supplement in your gut is not the same as GABA made inside your brain, behind the
blood-brain barrier, and whether oral GABA crosses that barrier at all is unresolved. a review of
the question found the studies "often contradictory," and floated that any real effect might not be
central at all, but peripheral, acting through the gut's own nervous system <CiteMarker n={2} />. if
that's true, the premise behind the whole product is shaky.

against that backdrop sits a single supportive trial: 40 people with insomnia symptoms, given 300 mg
a day of a fermented-rice-germ GABA, who fell asleep faster and slept more efficiently on a sleep
monitor than placebo <CiteMarker n={1} />. taken alone that looks encouraging. but it's one small
study, the groups were split 30 to 10, it used one specific proprietary product, and no one has
replicated it. and it can't settle the mechanism question: a positive result in a small, unbalanced
trial is exactly what you'd also expect from a peripheral effect, a placebo response, or chance.

so this is a **D**, not a C. a mixed-evidence C would mean the human data is genuinely conflicting on
a plausible mechanism. here the mechanism itself is in doubt, and the human data is one weak trial.
when the central premise might simply be false, a borderline grade should break down.
